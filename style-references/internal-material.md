# Style Reference Template: Internal Material

> **Document type:** Internal reference material — teaches domain knowledge to intelligent non-specialists.
> **Typical audience:** Analytically sophisticated professionals entering a new domain (e.g., advisory leads preparing for client conversations in an unfamiliar industry).
> **When to use:** Internal handbooks, briefing documents, and educational reference material where the reader needs to build genuine understanding — not just talking points. Covers documents where terminology must be defined contextually and every concept must be self-contained.

---

## Document Specification

**Type:** Modular reference handbook section
**Depth:** Detailed
**Purpose:** Inform
**Voice:** Analytical, Measured, Spacious

**Context:** Internal meeting-prep handbook for building conversational fluency with domain-specialist counterparts.

**Audience:** Intellectually sharp, analytically sophisticated professionals with no practical experience in the target domain. All domain terminology should be defined on first use through natural, contextual explanation woven into the prose. The text should be fully self-contained: a reader with no domain background should never need to stop and look something up.

**Key output:** Reader can discuss the domain's structural features, ecosystem tensions, and empirical evidence with the nuance expected of an informed counterpart.

---

## Voice Notes

- Academic textbook register: authoritative but accessible, teaches as it goes, never assumes the reader will just "get it"
- Define all domain terms on first use through contextual explanation, not parenthetical glosses
- Natural prose flow with varied sentence length and paragraph breathing
- No em dashes
- When a finding matters, give it a beat before moving on
- Every sentence must either (a) state a fact, (b) explain a concept, or (c) make a concrete analytical point. If a sentence does none of these, delete it.
- No meta-commentary about meeting utility or fluency signalling. The text teaches; the reader decides how to use it.
- No dramatic framing or rhetorical teasing. State conclusions when you reach them. Do not preview them, tease them, or announce that something important is coming.
- No "sounds wise" generalisations. If a sentence could apply to any industry or any report, it is too vague. Make it specific to the domain or cut it.
- Transitions should be functional, not rhetorical. "The next question is whether these returns justify the trade-offs" is functional. "This reshapes the conversation into something more pointed" is rhetorical. Use the first kind. Delete the second kind.
- No meeting coaching. Do not tell the reader how to use information in conversations, what signals fluency, or what position to take.
- No rhetorical questions or dramatic pivots. State findings and conclusions directly.
- No announcement sentences. Do not tell the reader what the section is about to do. Just do it.

---

## Sentence Construction

**Length norms:** Vary deliberately. Short declarative sentences (8–15 words) establish facts. Longer explanatory sentences (20–35 words) unpack concepts. Alternate to create breathing room — the "spacious" quality comes from not cramming.

**Structure:** Lead with the point, then explain. When introducing a concept, state what it is in the first sentence, then explain why it matters or how it works. Do not build suspense toward a definition.

**Complexity:** One idea per sentence. Explanatory sentences may use one subordinate clause to connect cause and effect. If a sentence needs two subordinate clauses, split it.

---

## Prohibited Patterns

- **Self-annotation:** No sentences about what the document does, how it is structured, or why a section exists.
- **Preambles:** No introductory paragraphs describing what a section will cover. Start with substance.
- **Meeting coaching:** No guidance on how to use information in conversations, what to say, what signals fluency, or what position to take.
- **Parenthetical definitions:** Do not define terms in parentheses — weave definitions into the prose naturally.
- **Assumed knowledge:** Never assume the reader knows domain terminology, acronyms, or institutional conventions. Define everything on first use.
- **Metadiscourse:** No "it is important to note," "notably," "significantly." If the content matters, its placement signals that.
- **False precision:** No quantitative claims that exceed the evidence precision.
- **Dramatic framing:** No "this changes everything," "what emerges is surprising," or similar rhetorical escalation.
- **Announcement sentences:** No "this section examines," "we now turn to," or "the following analysis shows."

---

## Audience Assumptions

The reader:
- Is analytically sophisticated — can follow complex arguments, statistical reasoning, and multi-step logic.
- Has no practical domain experience — every concept, term, and institutional convention must be introduced.
- Reads thoroughly (not selectively) — this is study material, not a reference to scan.
- Values depth over brevity — would rather understand a concept fully than get a summary.
- Will use this knowledge in conversations with domain specialists — accuracy and nuance matter more than simplification.

---

## Customization Notes

When applying this template to a specific document:

1. **Specify the domain:** Replace "domain" references with the actual field (e.g., "PE," "infrastructure finance," "Nordic real estate").
2. **Identify the strongest insight:** If the research surface one finding that is particularly meeting-ready, note it in the document specification so the prose gives it appropriate emphasis.
3. **Calibrate definition depth:** For highly technical domains, definitions may need worked examples. For moderately technical domains, contextual weaving is sufficient.
4. **Lock after first section:** Once the first section's prose is approved, this reference is locked for the document. Do not change voice mid-document.

Write customizations to the project's `{prose_output_dir}/style-reference.md` file, noting which template was used and what was changed.
